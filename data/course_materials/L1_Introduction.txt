NLP history (2)

• 1940s: machine translation
   • Possibility of using computers to translate documents between 
    natural human languages in March 1947 in a letter to the Warren Weaver
    cyberneticist Norbert Wiener.
   • "Translation" memorandum — Weaver's memorandum in 1949
• Ideas: more fruitful methods than any simplistic word-for-
 word approach
   • The problem of multiple meanings might be tackled by the 
    examination of immediate context
   • Formal logic: inspired by work on an early type of neural 
    networks by McCulloch and Pitts
   • Cryptographic methods were possibly applicable to translation 
    (from cryptography and information theory)
   • Linguistic universals underlying all human languages which could 
    be exploited to make the problem of translation more 
    straightforward

NLP history (4)

• 1960s and 1970s: many rule-based NLP systems 
• Chatterbot: 
   • In 1964, ELIZA, a “typewritten” comment and response 
    process, designed to imitate a psychiatrist using reflection 
    technique was developed.
   • PARRY was an early example of a chatbot, implemented in 
    1972 by psychiatrist Kenneth Colby.
• Natural language understanding
   • SHRDLU was developed by Terry Winograd at MIT in 1968–
    1970. The user carries on a conversation with the 
    computer, moving objects, naming collections and querying 
    the state of a simplified "blocks world", essentially a virtual 
    box filled with different blocks.

 NLP history (5)
• AI-“Winter”
   • In 1966, ALPAC halted the research funding in NLP, as no significant achievements in 
    contemporary NLP projects, especially in machine translation.
   • Until the 1980s, most NLP systems used complex, “handwritten” rules.
• 1980s and 1990s: statistical NLP
   • In the late 1980s, a revolution in NLP came about with the adoption of inchoate machine 
    learning algorithms and statistical models (e.g., decision trees and hidden Markov 
    models, supervised/unsupervised learning).
   • In the 1990s, pure statistics NLP methods have become remarkably valuable in keeping 
    pace with the tremendous flow of online text.

NLP history (6)

• 1990s – 2010s: statistical NLP towards neural NLP
  • In the 1990s, N-Grams were found useful in numerically recognizing and 
    tracking clumps of linguistic data.
  • In 1997, LSTM Recurrent Neural Net (RNN) models were introduced.
  • In 2001, Yoshio Bengio and his team proposed the first neural “language” 
    model, using a feed-forward neural network.
  • In the year 2011, Apple’s Siri became known as one of the world’s first 
    successful NLP/AI assistants to be used by general consumers.

How NLP works? 

• First step of NLP is text preprocessing, a data mining technique that 
 involves transforming the raw data into an understandable format.

• Before encoding text into numeric vector, we first need to clean the 
 text data .

• Process to prepare(or clean) text data before encoding is called text 
 preprocessing.

Preprocessing of data -    Tokenization

• Tokenization is the process of breaking up text document into 
 individual words called tokens.

• Tokens can be either words, characters, or sub-words.


                                                    33
Preprocessing of data -    Stop words removal

• Stop words are common words that do not contribute much of the 
 information in a text document. Words like ‘the’, ‘is’, ‘a’ have less 
 value and add noise to the text data.  “NLTK”


                                                    34
Preprocessing of data -    Stemming

• Stemming is the process of reducing a word to its stem/root word. It 
 reduces inflection in words (e.g. ‘help’, ’helping’, ’helped’, ’helpful’) to 
 their root form (e.g. ‘help’). It removes the morphological affixes from 
 words, leaving only the word stem.
• A crude heuristic process that chops off the ends of words in the 
 hope of correctly transforming words into its root form. So the words 
 “trouble”, “troubled” and “troubles” might actually be converted 
 to troubl instead of trouble because the ends were just chopped off.
• Porters Algorithm: empirically effective for English


                                                    35
Preprocessing of data -     Stemming

• The stem word may or may not be a valid word in the language. For 
  example ‘movi’ is the root word for ‘movie’, ‘emot’ is the root word for 
  ‘emotion’.


                                                      36
Preprocessing of data -    Lemmatization

• Lemmatization does the same thing as stemming, converting a word 
 to its root form but with one difference, i.e., the root word in this 
 case belongs to a valid word in the language. For example the word 
 caring would map to ‘care’ and not ‘car’ as the in case of stemming.

Tokenization  -  Word Tokenization
• Word Tokenization splits a piece of text into 
 individual words based on a certain delimiter.
• Drawbacks: Out Of Vocabulary (OOV) words, 
 these are new words encountered at testing that 
 do not exist in the vocabulary (set of tokens).
• Trick to solve OOV: 
  • Form the vocabulary with the Top K Frequent Words 
   and replace the rare words in training data 
   with unknown tokens (UNK).
  • mapping OOV to UNK tokens makes every OOV word 
   gets the same representation.
  • explodes the vocabulary.

Welcome to Byte Pair Encoding (BPE).

• Byte Pair Encoding (BPE) is a widely used tokenization method among 
 transformer-based models. BPE addresses the issues of Word and 
 Character Tokenizers:
  • BPE tackles OOV effectively. It segments OOV as subwords and represents the 
    word in terms of these subwords
  • The length of input and output sentences after BPE are shorter compared to 
    character tokenization

Welcome to Byte Pair Encoding (BPE).

• BPE is a word segmentation algorithm that merges the most 
 frequently occurring character or character sequences iteratively. 
 Here is a step by step guide to learn BPE.
• Steps to learn BPE
  • Split the words in the corpus into characters after appending </w>
  • Initialize the vocabulary with unique characters in the corpus
  • Compute the frequency of a pair of characters or character sequences in 
    corpus
  • Merge the most frequent pair in corpus
  • Save the best pair to the vocabulary
  • Repeat steps 3 to 5 for a certain number of iterations
